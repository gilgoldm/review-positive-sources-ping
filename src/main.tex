\begin{center}
{\Large \textbf{Referee Report}}\\[0.5em]
\textit{Computational resolution limits for super-resolution of positive sources}
\end{center}

This paper studies computational resolution limits for super-resolution of positive sources from noisy band-limited Fourier data.
The main quantitative question is how small the minimum separation may be, as a function of $\Omega$, $\sigma$, $m_{\min}$, and $n$,
while still allowing stable detection of the correct number of sources or stable recovery of their locations.
In one dimension, the authors define the corresponding resolution limits for number detection and support recovery,
prove explicit upper and lower bounds, and then extend the theory to higher dimensions.

In my view, the main contribution of the paper is the lower bound for the
number-detection problem in the positive-source case, namely the lower bound on the minimum
separation required to detect the correct number of sources.
More precisely, Theorem~2.2 constructs an $n$-point positive measure and an $(n-1)$-point positive measure whose
Fourier data differ by less than $2\sigma$ and whose minimum separation is
\[
\frac{2e^{-1}}{\Omega}\left(\frac{2\sigma}{m_{\min}}\right)^{\!1/(2n-2)}.
\]
This is the theorem that, in my opinion, carries the main novelty of the paper.
The result is useful because it shows that positivity does not improve the asymptotic SRF--SNR
law for number detection: the exponent remains the same as in the general-source case.
What positivity does provide is better constants.
While it may be possible to adapt previously published techniques
to the postive case, explicit constants of this quality are not
easily attained.

The support-recovery lower bound, the multidimensional extension, the instability examples,
and the numerical phase-transition sections are useful complements.
They strengthen the paper and make the picture more complete,
yet the key new point is still the positive-source lower bound for number detection.

At the level of proof, the lower-bound argument is based on the framework developed earlier in [32].
The main ingredients are the same: one constructs an underdetermined Vandermonde system,
exploits vanishing moments, controls the coefficients via inverse-Vandermonde/Lagrange formulas,
and estimates the Fourier tail using factorial/Stirling bounds.
The new ingredient is the additional analysis needed to enforce positivity,
especially the sign pattern that allows the null vector to be split into two positive measures.
I therefore view the argument as a nontrivial positivity-adapted refinement of [32],
rather than as a completely different method.
This does not diminish the value of the result, but it should be described accurately.

The paper is generally clear and well organized.
My main recommendation is that the authors should
state much more explicitly that the principal novelty,
relative to the previous papers, is the lower bound for number detection in the positive case.

\textbf{Recommendation:} \emph{Accept subject to minor revisions.}

\textbf{Comments to the authors}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \textbf{State the main novelty more clearly.}
In my opinion, the introduction and Section~1.2 should say explicitly that the principal new result is the lower bound
for the number-detection problem for positive sources.
At present, the paper presents a broader list of contributions,
but the real novelty relative to the earlier papers is more specific.

\item \textbf{Compare directly with the general-source lower bounds from [32].}
It would be helpful to state the corresponding one-dimensional lower bounds from [32]
for general (not necessarily positive) sources and compare them explicitly with the present positive-source bounds.
The asymptotic exponents are the same, while the constants are better in the positive case.
A short direct comparison would make the contribution much easier to assess.

\item \textbf{Rewrite the main bounds also in SRF/SNR form.}
For comparison with previous super-resolution results, it would be useful to restate the one-dimensional bounds in the form
\[
\mathrm{SNR} \gtrsim (C\,\mathrm{SRF})^{2n-2}
\]
for number detection and
\[
\mathrm{SNR} \gtrsim (C\,\mathrm{SRF})^{2n-1}
\]
for stable support recovery.
This may make the fact that the problem is essentially scale-invariant in $\Omega$ and $\Delta$
as long as the multiplication remains the same, more transparent.

\item \textbf{Describe the proof method more accurately relative to [32].}
I suggest moderating the language around ``our new technique''.
The lower-bound construction is, in my reading, built on the framework of [32],
with the main new step being the analysis that enforces positivity.
I would recommend saying this explicitly.

\item \textbf{Sharpen the literature review slightly.}
The discussion of the ``theoretical possibility'' of super-resolution for positive
sources could be positioned more carefully relative to classical Prony-type identifiability
and related exact-recovery statements when enough measurements are available.
I do not think this requires a large rewrite, but one clarifying sentence would improve the historical framing.

\item \textbf{Add a short remark about the real-line formulation.}
The one-dimensional results are not essentially tied to the particular interval $I(n,\Omega)$;
they can be recentered and viewed on the real line.
A brief remark to this effect would improve exposition.

\item \textbf{Add a practical remark after Theorem~2.3.}
After Theorem~2.3, it would be helpful to record explicitly that combining the separation
condition with the pointwise localization estimate yields an error bound showing that the
recovered locations remain within a fixed fraction of the minimum separation.
This is a useful practical consequence of the theorem.

\item \textbf{Minor presentation issues.}
I support the smaller comments marked in the annotated PDF, including writing the main thresholds in SRF form,
using standard notation consistently, and replacing some references by hyperlinks/cross-references where appropriate.
\end{enumerate}

Overall, I think this is a good paper.
The main result is useful, the constants are explicit and reasonably sharp,
and the manuscript is suitable for publication once the above expository revisions are made.
